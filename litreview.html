Literature

Lit review

Five strands of literature this project sits against — where the pipeline's design choices come from, and where the r = 0.09 finding sits relative to what the field would predict.

Bucket 1

Pedestrian detection methods

Automated pedestrian detection has advanced steadily since YOLOv3 established single-pass bounding-box regression, through to YOLOv8's anchor-free head. Attribute-recognition benchmarks like PA-100K and PETA, paired with EfficientNet backbones, made gender and age classification from crops feasible at scale. The consistent limitation across this body of work: every dataset and evaluation protocol reflects Western or East Asian surveillance environments — fixed cameras, temperate climates, Western clothing norms. Even the first proof that GSV imagery could substitute for manual pedestrian counts (Yin et al., 2015) was tested in low-density US cities. Nothing in this literature confronts dense motorbike traffic, tropical midday light, or hijab silhouettes at once — the specific combination this pipeline's rider gate and domain-adapted classifier were built to handle.

Bucket 2

Street view imagery for urban analysis

Biljecki & Ito's (2021) review of 619 papers maps street-view imagery's use across ten urban analytics domains and identifies deep learning as the field's dominant method — but flags pedestrian attribute recognition as an underdeveloped use case, with sparse coverage of Global South cities. Gebru et al. (2017) showed demographic attributes could be inferred from 50 million GSV images across US cities; Neuhold et al.'s (2017) Mapillary Vistas dataset, which trains this project's segmentation model, established geographically diverse street-scene segmentation as a research resource. This project sits inside that tradition while filling a specific gap the review identifies: gendered pedestrian presence and streetscape quality measured together, at full-city scale, in a tropical Global South megacity.

Bucket 3

Walkability and pedestrian studies

Ewing & Cervero's (2010) meta-analysis of ~60 studies is the field's foundational synthesis: walking responds most to land-use diversity, destination density, and connectivity — the "5Ds" framework. That literature, including Jakarta-specific work (Lo's 2011 walkability dissertation, Sihombing et al.'s 2022 CBD study), predicts pedestrian volume should track physical infrastructure quality fairly closely. That prediction is exactly what this project's r = 0.09 result complicates: for female pedestrians specifically, presence turns out to track economic activity far more than street quality, a departure from the built-environment literature's usual expectation worth reading against this backdrop.

Bucket 4

Gender and urban mobility

Women's public-space presence responds to safety conditions in ways physical infrastructure alone doesn't explain, and is further shaped by the "mobility of care" (Sánchez de Madariaga, 2013) — the fragmented, multi-stop trip patterns that care work generates, concentrating women's movement near markets, schools, and services rather than commuter corridors. In Indonesia, where the informal sector is roughly 60% of employment (ILO, 2023), women are disproportionately concentrated in market trading — precisely the dense, mixed-use street activity this project's GPI is built to capture. Read against this literature, the GPI–SQI orthogonality is neither anomalous nor surprising: it reflects an already-documented pattern, measured here at a scale and spatial resolution the literature hasn't previously had access to.

Bucket 5

Domain adaptation in computer vision

Pan & Yang's (2010) taxonomy frames exactly the setting this pipeline faces: a transductive transfer problem, where source (PA-100K, PETA) and target (Jakarta) share the same task but not the same distribution. The field's dominant unsupervised solutions — DANN (Ganin et al., 2016), ADDA (Tzeng et al., 2017), CORAL (Sun & Saenko, 2016) — align feature spaces without labelled target data, but assume the domain gap is modest. GeoNet (Kalluri et al., 2023) shows that assumption strains even across geographies with similar camera setups. Jakarta's gap — surveillance to panoramic street-level imagery, temperate to tropical light, Western clothing to hijab silhouettes — is wide enough that this project uses supervised sequential fine-tuning (PA-100K → PETA → 8,400+ locally annotated Jakarta crops) rather than unsupervised alignment alone, trading annotation cost for reliability on a target domain the literature's standard tools weren't built for.
